They Were Here First

Archaeological records suggest that over a million Native Americans may once have lived in Utah, as early as 12,000 years ago. Among the most sophisticated were the Anasazi and Fremont cultures which flourished between 1,500 and 600 years ago. They lived in masonry structures, made complex basketry and pottery, and developed a maize-bean-squash-based agriculture. Numerous explorers and trappers ventured through Utah between 1776 and 1847, making contact and trading with the Native American peoples. Northeastern Utah was home to the Shoshone and Ute. Both tribes were hunter-gatherers who had adopted the horse and buffalo of the Plains Indians. They lived in brush shelters or skin tepees and traveled in extended family units with seasonal band congregations. When the Mormon migration began in 1847, there were more than 20,000 Native Americans living in Utah proper. Early Mormon settlers in this area encountered the Cumumba or Weber Ute band. The settlers' journals indicate that, by and large, natives and settlers coexisted peacefully.
